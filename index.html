Learning to Switch

Dec 27th, 2014 2:44 am

Ways to switch roles in contra, by figure

foo

I recently started switching roles on each progression, and was annoyed by how much I had to rediscover on my own. Like much of contra, switching is mostly taught informally, with friends passing tips back and forth. This is my attempt to summarize what I’ve learned in the first year or so of trying to switch.

I assume you don’t need to be convinced to try switching. Instead, I’ll start with general comments about learning to switch, and then describe a few ways to switch during common figures.

Learning to switch

I assume your local dance doesn’t have a lot of people who switch. If it did, you should go talk with them instead of reading this. Or better yet, ask them to dance!

I recommend learning at a large dance weekend or other experienced venue. You’ll be more likely to find a partner who switches, and your neighbors will be on average more experienced dancers who can recover from your mistakes.

I’ve found complicated choreography to be less of a hindrance than unhelpful neighbors. Some people will think they’re the one’s who have switched, and try to un-switch. Some people will think you’ve done it to yourselves accidentally, and try to un-switch you. I don’t have as much trouble with either of these at more experienced dances.

If you’re looking for a partner, some people who like to switch have dance buttons that advertise this. Mark Galipeau from SF originally distributed a white one that asked, “Would you like to lead or follow?” His second batch has a rainbow background and instead asks, “What’s your preference? I dance both roles.” A third kind I’ve seen is a white button with a black pointer that can be moved to indicate “Lead,” “Follow,” or “Switch.” I don’t know who made that one.

Absent an explicit sign like a dance button, most people won’t take it amiss if you ask, “Do you ever switch roles?”.

At first, I would go through the line once as a gent to learn the choreography and switch at the end. Later I started switching on every progression, on the same figure each time. Now I switch pretty much without forethought, averaging once per progression.

I find it helps to stick to the convention that gents holds their palms up and ladies their palms down. This serves both as a reminder to me and as a signal to my partner.

How to switch, by figure

For brevity, I use male pronouns for the person who starts in the gent’s place, and female pronouns for the person who starts in the lady’s place. Of course, I believe it is acceptable to for men to dance the lady’s role and vice versa.

The easiest way is to enter a balance and swing with my palms turned the other way. This gives my partner four beats to notice the switch, and then I have eight to twelve beats to remember my partner’s part. If there is no balance, I instead enter the swing switched, which is almost as good.

Swing

  • Toward the end of the swing, lady passes the gent’s left hand from her right to her left hand and puts her right hand behind his shoulder as the gent does in ballroom. They now have left hands joined between them about chin level. The lady twirls the gent out with her left hand to end switched.

  • Both dancers swing in their original roles, but exit with the lady stopping on the left and letting the gent roll away into the lady’s place.

  • Swing in a symmetric hold and exit with the lady twirling the gent into the right-hand place. E.g.,

    • right hands as in ballroom and left hands clasped overhead; standard under-arm twirl with left hands joined above
    • right hands as in ballroom, left arms around outside the other’s right elbow, and left hands clasped below; twirl by disengaging the right arm and lifting the joined left hands above
    • cross-hand hold, right hands above left; twirl out as if twirling out of a skater’s promenade

  • In the middle of a ballroom swing, gent twirls the lady out and catches her again, in switched position.

Balance and swing

If my partner knows the convention of gents turning their palms up and ladies their palms down, I often signal a switch by balancing with my hands turned the other way.

  • Gent offers the lady a right-handed balance, boxes the gnat, then passes the lady behind him. He finishes with his right hand behind his back placing the lady’s hand on his left shoulder blade and his left hand dropping onto her right shoulder to enter a ballroom swing, switched.

    • The exit position is the entry position for a ripcord twirl, a.k.a. a swing-dancer’s Apache twirl-out.

  • Gent lifts his right hand holding the lady’s left hand and turns under to enter a cuddle-up swing, switched. If the gent leads this, it’s easier to lead the lady’s hand if he balances palms up.

Long lines

  • If partner is on the side, roll away with a half chasse as long lines retreat

  • If partner is across, swap places with a half-turn of a barrel hold swing before long lines retreat.

By skipping figures that switch partners

  • Substitute a balance for California twirl, star through, box the gnat, or swat the flea.

  • Turn alone instead of as a couple (or vice versa).

  • For interrupted square through two, skip the partner pull by. I rarely have success putting in a balance, probably because a pull by is two beats and the usual balance takes four.

  • I don’t often try to add half-turns to allemande, gypsy, and do si do. Many dances allow three or four beats per half-turn, so adding a half-turn usually feels rushed.

Contra corners

  • Instead of turning partner halfway, riccochet while twirling over one’s right shoulder into an allemande left with partner’s corner.

  • If entering via a half figure eight, instead lead and cast, ending on the same side.

Petronella

  • Whoever is on the right rolls the other from left to right, without moving. They end as if both had stepped to the right and then switched.

    • A variation that’s harder to lead without advance notice is to twirl whoever is moving. The twirl is over his left shoulder.

Ladies chain

  • Taking a courtesy turn position, the gent stops the lady as they are both facing out, and twirls them both in place to face in, switched.

    • If the tempo is slow enough, one can often sneak in a shallow side-dip before the twirl.
    • Or courtesy turn a full circle to face back out, then twirl.
    • Or courtesy turn, ending with lady on the right and both facing in, then twirl the lady into the gent’s place.

  • Gent leads one twirl with his right hand lifting the lady’s left, then rolls the lady from right to left with a half-chasse for himself. This often requires a slow tempo.

Hey

  • If the call is to pass partner, riccochet instead. This is easy in the middle and harder at the ends, but can be done at both.

Rory O’Moore

  • If the call is to balance and slide past partner, switch hands coming out of the last balance and allemande instead.

    • E.g., if the call is balance right, balance left, slide right, then one would instead balance right and left, and allemande left one turn.

    • The timing is tight enough that this is virtually impossible to teach in the moment. If I haven’t achieved a cross-hands grip by the fourth beat of the last balance, the switch will probably fail.
